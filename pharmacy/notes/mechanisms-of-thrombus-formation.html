Mechanisms of thrombus formation

Authors: Bruce Furie, M.D., Barbara Furie, Ph.D.

Created 2016-01-04 by Michael L. Bernauer

Background

  • Vessel wall injury + extravasion of blood initiate events that form a clot
  • Platelents are recruited to the site of injury
  • Tissue factor results in generation of thrombin and fibrin
  • Hemostasis is normally in check; patho states can overwhelm regulatory mechanisms leading to thrombosis
  • Venous thrombosis is second leading cause of death in patients with cancer

Formation of a platelet thrombus

  • Inner lining of vessel wall is endothelium; contains 3 thromboregulators nitric oxide, prostacycline and ectonucleotidase CD39* which protect against thrombus formation
  • Collagen and tissue factor become exposed during injury initiating formation of thrombus
  • Collagen → platelet activation
  • Tissue factor → thrombin formation
  • Thrombin converts fibrinogen → fibrin + activates platelets

Two pathways to platelet activation

  • Two pathways to platelet activation included collegen pathway and tissue factor/thrombin pathway
  • One pathway may predominate depending on disease; end result (platelet activation) is the same

Collagen pathway

  • Platelet glycoprotein VI interacts with collagen
  • Platelet glycoprotein Ib-V-IV interacts with collagen-bound von Willebrand factor
  • This results in platelet adhesion to site of injury; both steps are required for adhesion

Tissue factor pathyway

  • Tissue factor complexes with factor VIIa which activates factors IX → proteolytic cascade → generating thrombin
  • Thrombin cleaves protease-activated receptor 4 (Par1) on platelet surface causing activation
  • Active platelets release ADP, serotonin, and thromboxane A2

Due to these overlapping mechanism inhibitino of GPVI in the collagen pathway or inhibition of clotting enzymes in the tissue-factor pathway may not fully inhibit platelet activation

Propagation of platelet thrombus

  • Developing thrombus recruits and activates unstimulated platelets
  • αIIbβ is a platelet integrin that is activated by conformational change in activated platelets bound to injured cell wall
  • αIIbβ3 binds with fibrinogin (low shear rates) and von Willebran factor (high shear)
  • During platelet activation, late signaling events enhcance platelet-platelet affinity
    • growth-arrest-specific gene 6, CD40, ephrinEph, and signaling lymphocyte activation molecule create protective environment in platelet interstices stabilizing the thrombus
  • Platelet activation → alpha-granules (proteins)/dense granules (ADP/Ca2+ release → thrombus formation
  • ADP stimulates platelet activation through P2Y1 and P2Y12 receptors

Tissue factor

  • Functions to promote:
    • Blood coagulation
    • Angiogenesis
    • Tumor progression
    • Metastasis
  • Expressed in adventitia and medial smooth muscle cells of vessel wall
  • Tissues factor also found in circulating microparticles.
  • Activated platelets express P-selectin which binds to circulating microparticles containing tissue factor
  • This facilitates fribrin propagation when vessel-wall injury is limited to endothelial cell-activation
  • Microparticle tissue factor is 'encrypted' as to not lead to blood coagulation
